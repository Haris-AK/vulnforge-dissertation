# Background landscape for an LLM-driven, ephemeral vulnerability-generation training platform

Existing hands-on cybersecurity training is dominated by **static, pre-authored challenges that expose every learner to the same vulnerability and the same flag**, and no mainstream platform yet uses generative AI to synthesise runnable vulnerabilities paired with a context-aware tutor. This background synthesises three literatures that frame the dissertation's contribution: the incumbent Capture-the-Flag (CTF) and skills-lab ecosystem, the rapidly maturing use of Large Language Models (LLMs) in offensive, defensive and educational cybersecurity, and the sandboxing/orchestration substrate that makes ephemeral, deliberately vulnerable environments safe to deploy at scale. Each literature independently supports a common conclusion: the pedagogical value of CTF-style learning is attenuated by content staticity and write-up proliferation; LLMs are already demonstrably capable of generating, solving and reasoning about vulnerable code; and mature container, microVM and user-space-kernel technologies provide the defence-in-depth needed to run attacker-selected exploit code in multi-tenant settings. The dissertation sits at the unoccupied intersection of these three areas.

## The existing CTF and training-platform ecosystem

The commercial and community platforms that dominate hands-on cybersecurity education were built over the last fifteen years around a shared architectural assumption: a challenge is authored once, reviewed, and published so that every user encounters an identical artefact. This assumption underlies the operational economics of the sector and also underlies its most commonly cited pedagogical weakness.

**Hack The Box (HTB)**, founded in 2017 by Haris Pylarinos, James Hooker and Aris Zikopoulos, became the sector's flagship through a mix of community-authored vulnerable VMs ("Machines") and in-house enterprise content. HTB runs on a freemium-plus-enterprise model (with a $55 M Series B in early 2023) and publishes one new Machine per week, retiring another simultaneously so that roughly twenty remain free-tier accessible. Content types include Machines, jeopardy-style Challenges, multi-host Pro Labs (Dante, Offshore, RastaLabs, Cybernetics, Zephyr), Fortresses, Endgames, the Sherlocks blue-team scenarios, Seasons, and HTB Academy with its CPTS/CBBH/CDSA certifications. Learners connect via per-user OpenVPN into private 10.x lab subnets or use the browser-streamed Pwnbox (launched May 2020). Authoring is community-driven — third-party "box creators" submit a VM and write-up, HTB's internal content team QAs and publishes — but once a Machine goes live, **its services, CVE chain and 32-character flag hashes are fixed for every player**. IppSec's YouTube walkthroughs and 0xdf's blog, published on the day a machine is retired, convert solved content into a permanent shortcut. A September 2025 acquisition of LetsDefend introduced defensive labs and an announced roadmap for "AI-augmented SOC simulation," but HTB's AI features to date (the Guided Mode beta) act as tutors over static challenges rather than as generators of new ones.

**TryHackMe**, founded in 2018 by Ben Spring and Ashu Savani, pursued a more scaffolded, beginner-oriented pedagogy organised around "Rooms" — self-contained labs that mix markdown instruction, exact-match answer fields and a deployable VM target grouped into learning paths such as Pre Security, Jr Penetration Tester, SOC Level 1, Red Teaming and Cyber Defense. Delivery mirrors HTB's VPN/AttackBox model. The platform now claims over 3 million registered users and runs well over 900 rooms, a mix of in-house and moderated community authorship. **Every deployed instance of a given room contains the same vulnerabilities and the same exact-match flags**, with the narrow exception of some King-of-the-Hill rooms that rotate flags hourly while leaving the underlying vulnerability static. TryHackMe has added rooms teaching LLM attacks but does not use generative AI to synthesise per-user content.

**picoCTF**, operated by Carnegie Mellon's CyLab and the Plaid Parliament of Pwning since 2013, is the largest student-oriented CTF and is entirely free. Its annual ten-day March competition is complemented by a year-round picoGym archive with cumulatively over 900,000 learners by 2025. Problems are jeopardy-style across web, crypto, reversing, pwn, forensics and general-skills categories, delivered through browser and Docker-backed challenge instances. Critically, **it was the picoCTF team itself** — Burket, Chapman, Becker, Ganas and Brumley — that published the seminal paper on static-content abuse: *Automatic Problem Generation for Capture-the-Flag Competitions* (USENIX 3GSE 2015) measured rampant flag-sharing during picoCTF 2014 and proposed Automatic Problem Generation (APG) to mitigate it. APG is still only applied to a subset of templated crypto and web problems; the bulk of picoGym remains static.

**VulnHub** (g0tmi1k, 2010; acquired by Offensive Security in 2024) occupies the extreme of staticity — users simply download community-contributed OVA/VMDK images and run them locally, so every user executes the same disk image with the same `/root/flag.txt`. **OverTheWire** (circa 2005) goes further still: its Bandit, Narnia, Natas and Leviathan wargames use SSH accounts with passwords that have remained constant for years and are trivially searchable. **Root-Me** (2010) offers more than 600 jeopardy challenges and uniquely forbids public write-ups by rule, a norm-based rather than architectural response to the same problem.

Blue-team-focused **CyberDefenders** and **LetsDefend** (the latter acquired by HTB in 2025) simulate SOC workflows with downloadable pcap/memory/log evidence and fixed-answer questionnaires. **PortSwigger's Web Security Academy**, launched in 2019 by the Burp Suite team, represents a technical advance in that it provisions a per-user cloned web application for each of its 250+ labs (PortSwigger reports "hundreds of thousands of custom generated legally-hackable websites each month"), but **the vulnerability class, the codebase and the canonical solution are identical across users**; per-user provisioning serves isolation, not content variation. **PentesterLab** (Louis Nyffenegger, ~2013) delivers roughly 700 heavily curated exercises each tied to one real-world CVE. Enterprise platforms **Immersive Labs** (Bristol, 2017, James Hadley ex-GCHQ), **RangeForce**, and **SANS NetWars** (Ed Skoudis's Counter Hack team, ~2009) package thousands of labs for corporate workforces; content refresh cycles are typically annual and authoring is in-house, but the intra-cycle content is static. **CTFd** (Kevin Chung, 2017) is the open-source scoring and orchestration framework behind hundreds of university and corporate CTFs, including the current generation of picoCTF; its core challenge types store a static flag string or a regex, and while plug-ins such as CTFd-whale can launch per-user Docker instances, the vulnerability inside those instances is identical across tenants.

A small academic literature has diagnosed the **write-up and flag-sharing problem** directly. Vykopal, Švábenský and Chang's *Benefits and Pitfalls of Using Capture the Flag Games in University Courses* (SIGCSE 2020) names plagiarism, scoring gaming and limited scaffolding as the three structural weaknesses of CTF-based assessment. Švábenský et al.'s 2021 analysis in *Computers & Security* quantifies the scale of public write-ups. Chapman, Burket and Brumley (3GSE 2014) and Cole (ITiCSE 2022) establish the positive pedagogical case. A narrow but important tradition anticipates the dissertation's thesis through template-based procedural generation: Burket et al.'s **APG** (2015), Feng's metamorphic reversing binaries (3GSE 2015), Hulin et al.'s **AutoCTF / LAVA-based bug injection** (USENIX WOOT 2017), Schreuders et al.'s **Security Scenario Generator (SecGen)** (USENIX ASE 2017) — the closest pre-LLM precedent, composing randomised vulnerable rich-scenario VMs from Ruby/Puppet templates — Eckroth et al.'s **ALPACA** (ACM SE 2019), and most recently **AgentCyTE** (arXiv 2025), which uses an LLM agent to patch topology XML files and integrate Vulhub CVEs into scenarios. **None of this work synthesises novel vulnerable code or configurations end-to-end, and none pairs generated instances with a context-aware tutor.** Existing AI features on commercial platforms (HTB Guided Mode, LetsDefend's announced AI SOC, Immersive's AI-generated crisis media) act on the learner side as hint or adaptation mechanisms over pre-existing static content.

## LLMs in cybersecurity: generators, solvers, tutors

Three distinct scholarly conversations have grown up around LLMs and security, and the dissertation draws on all three.

**LLMs as authors of insecure code.** OpenAI's 2021 Codex release (Chen et al., arXiv:2107.03374) established that transformer LLMs trained on public code could synthesise functional programs from docstrings. The security implications were first systematically measured by **Pearce, Ahmad, Tan, Dolan-Gavitt and Karri in *Asleep at the Keyboard? Assessing the Security of GitHub Copilot's Code Contributions*** (IEEE S&P 2022), whose 89-scenario, 1,689-program evaluation across MITRE's 2021 Top-25 CWEs found that **approximately 40% of Copilot completions contained exploitable weaknesses**, concentrated in SQL injection, buffer overflows and use-after-free. Sandoval et al. (*Lost at C*, USENIX Security 2023) tempered this with a controlled N=58 C-programming study showing AI-assisted code was no more than 10 % more likely to contain critical bugs than unassisted code. The more alarming empirical result came from **Perry, Srivastava, Kumar and Boneh's *Do Users Write More Insecure Code with AI Assistants?*** (ACM CCS 2023, N=47), which found AI-assisted participants produced significantly less secure code on four of five tasks and were simultaneously more likely to believe their insecure code was secure. Benchmarks now standardise the measurement: **SecurityEval** (Siddiq & Santos, MSR4P&S 2022; 121 Python prompts / 69 CWEs), **LLMSecEval** (Tony et al., MSR 2023), Meta's **CyberSecEval 1–4** "Purple Llama" suite including AutoPatchBench and CyberSOCEval, **CodeLMSec** (Hajipour et al., SaTML 2024), and the executable-oracle-based **CWEval** (Peng et al., 2025). The cumulative finding is that LLMs reliably emit CWE-listed bugs and that this propensity can be **deliberately harnessed** to produce vulnerable artefacts — precisely the affordance the dissertation exploits.

**LLMs as offensive agents and vulnerability discoverers.** The offensive literature has moved rapidly from chat prompting to agentic tool use. **PentestGPT** (Deng et al., USENIX Security 2024, Distinguished Artifact Award) introduced a tripartite Reasoning/Generation/Parsing agent around a "Pentesting Task Tree" and reported a 228.6% improvement over raw GPT-3.5 on HackTheBox/VulnHub targets, with later XBOW validation at 86.5% task success on 104 benchmarks. **HackingBuddyGPT** (Happe & Cito, ESEC/FSE 2023) was the first published autonomous LLM pentester; their 2025 *Empirical Software Engineering* follow-up measured GPT-4-Turbo exploiting 33–83% of curated Linux privilege-escalation scenarios, comparable to human testers at 75%. Further agents — AutoAttacker, Nebula, PenHeal, HackSynth, ARACNE, AutoPentester (arXiv:2510.05605, 2025), VulnBot, and Fang et al.'s zero-day exploit teams — populate this subfield. On the fuzzing side, **TitanFuzz** (Deng et al., ISSTA 2023) used Codex/InCoder as seed generator and mutator for deep-learning-library fuzzing, achieving 30–50 % higher coverage than SOTA fuzzers and discovering 65 bugs (41 novel); **FuzzGPT** (Deng et al., ICSE 2024) primed LLMs with bug-triggering corpora and found 76 additional bugs including 49 previously unknown. The crowning validation is the **DARPA AI Cyber Challenge (AIxCC)**: at the August 2025 DEF CON 33 finals, **Team Atlanta's ATLANTIS** won the $4 M grand prize with competitor systems collectively discovering 54 of 63 synthetic vulnerabilities (86%) and patching 43 (68%) across 54 million lines of open-source code, plus 18 real production vulnerabilities. All seven finalist Cyber Reasoning Systems are being open-sourced. LLMs have also been shown to solve CTFs: Tann et al. (2023), Yang et al.'s *Language Agents as Hackers* (NeurIPS R0-FoMo 2023) with InterCode-CTF, Shao et al.'s NYU CTF Bench (NeurIPS 2024 D&B) and EnIGMA agent, and Zhang et al.'s **Cybench** (ICLR 2025). Underpinning all these systems is the **ReAct** prompting pattern (Yao et al., ICLR 2023) and tool-use/function-calling frameworks such as LangChain and Microsoft's AutoGen.

**LLMs as tutors, and their failure modes.** A third literature treats LLMs as educational agents. The canonical design tension is between the LLM as answer-giver and as Socratic guide. **SocraticAI** (Sunil & Thakkar, arXiv:2512.03501, 2025) is representative: an LLM wrapped with authentication, query validation, reflection prompts and a **RAG layer grounded in course material** to enforce scaffolded hints and prevent solution leakage. Khanmigo (Khan Academy, 2023), Liffiton et al.'s **CodeHelp** (ICER 2023), and Denny et al.'s ITiCSE 2024 studies demonstrate similar patterns in programming education. In security education specifically, CSET's 2023 Georgetown analysis of ChatGPT as CTF preparation, Tann et al.'s (2023) academic-integrity findings, and open-source prototypes such as CTFMentorGPT explore hint-based tutoring over static challenges. Three technical risks dominate this literature and bear directly on the dissertation's design. **Prompt injection and jailbreaking** — documented by Greshake et al. (2023), AutoAttacker's role-play prefix, and CyberSecEval 2's 25–50% success rates against SOTA models — are acute for a tutor that must hold privileged knowledge of the flag. **Hallucination** produces plausible-but-incorrect commands and CVE references that can teach students wrong mental models. **Context-window saturation** over long sessions degrades solving agents (Shao et al., 2025) and would equally degrade a tutor. The consensus mitigation across SocraticAI, later PentestGPT versions, VulnBot and AutoPentester is **Retrieval-Augmented Generation** (Lewis et al., NeurIPS 2020), combined with function-calling guardrails and system-prompt constraints such as explicit refusal of strings prefixed `flag{`. Three recent surveys — **Yao et al.** (*High-Confidence Computing* 4(2), 2024), **Xu et al.** (ACM TOSEM 2025, screening >40,000 papers and analysing 185), and **Motlagh et al.** (arXiv:2405.14487, 2024) — orient the field and jointly document a paradigm shift toward autonomous multi-step security agents.

The three threads remain largely disjoint. LLMs as offensive solvers match junior human testers; LLMs as benchmarked insecure-code generators reliably emit CWEs; LLMs as generic tutors are being wrapped in Socratic and RAG scaffolds. **No substantial published work combines the three into a system that generates fresh, per-session, runnable, validated vulnerabilities inside sandboxed containers as pedagogical artefacts and pairs each generated instance with a context-aware LLM tutor that has privileged, grounded knowledge of exactly what was created.** AIxCC generates synthetic bugs, but for benchmarking autonomous defenders; TitanFuzz and FuzzGPT generate bug-triggering inputs, but not educational challenges; SecurityEval-class benchmarks provide static vulnerable snippets without a tutor; PentestGPT and its kin are solvers rather than generators or tutors.

## Sandboxing and orchestration as the enabling substrate

Running deliberately vulnerable, LLM-authored code for untrusted learners imposes an asymmetric security requirement: the learner must be granted attack surface against a weak target, but escape to the host or to other tenants must be structurally prevented, and the target's *image and configuration* must themselves be treated as potentially adversarial because a generative model produced them. The authoritative secondary source is **NIST SP 800-190, *Application Container Security Guide*** (Souppaya, Morello & Scarfone, 2017; updated 2021), which organises risks into image, registry, orchestrator, container and host-OS tiers and recommends stateless, ephemeral operation — explicitly, that containers should be "destroyed and replaced" rather than mutated in place. This aligns directly with the dissertation's ephemeral design.

**Docker and its OCI-compliant runc/containerd stack** dominate CTF-style lab delivery because images are portable, layered and start in sub-seconds. Isolation is provided by Linux kernel primitives — namespaces (pid, net, mnt, uts, ipc, user, cgroup), cgroups v1/v2, capabilities, seccomp-bpf (whose Docker default blocks roughly 44 syscalls including `mount`, `reboot`, `setns` and `kexec_load`), and AppArmor/SELinux mandatory access control. HackTheBox explicitly uses Docker for its internet-facing non-Machine challenges while reserving VMs for full Machines. The canonical container-escape vulnerabilities a dissertation must cite are: **CVE-2019-5736**, the runc `/proc/self/exe` overwrite that allows a root-in-container process to replace the host's runc binary (patched in runc ≥ 1.0-rc7 / Docker 18.09.2; mitigated completely by SELinux enforcing mode and by user-namespace remapping); **CVE-2024-21626 "Leaky Vessels"** (Snyk, January 2024), an order-of-operations flaw in runc ≤ 1.1.11 that leaks a host-filesystem file descriptor via a crafted `WORKDIR /proc/self/fd/N`, enabling arbitrary read and container escape (patched in runc 1.1.12, alongside BuildKit companion CVEs 2024-23651/23652/23653); **CVE-2022-0847 "Dirty Pipe"** (Kellermann, March 2022), an uninitialised `pipe_buffer.flags` bug in Linux ≥ 5.8 that permits unprivileged overwrite of the page cache regardless of filesystem permissions; and **CVE-2016-5195 "Dirty COW"**, whose scumjr vDSO-based PoC demonstrated a full non-privileged container-to-host escape. Configuration-based escapes — privileged mode, bind-mounted `docker.sock`, added `CAP_SYS_ADMIN`/`CAP_SYS_MODULE`, `--pid=host`, cgroup v1 `release_agent` misuse — remain dominant in practice and are the top enumerated risks in the **Container Security Survey** (Jarkas, Ko et al., *ACM Computing Surveys*, January 2025, DOI 10.1145/3715001), which catalogues over 200 container CVEs across 47 exploit categories.

These repeated kernel-boundary escapes underwrite the consensus that **containers alone are not a security boundary when running deliberately-untrusted code**. The rebuttal is to move the boundary: either to a user-space kernel or to a hypervisor. **gVisor** (Google, OCI runtime `runsc`) re-implements ~211 of ~319 Linux syscalls in Go, forwarding a small filtered set to the host kernel through an additional seccomp filter; empirical overheads from *The True Cost of Containing* (Young et al., HotCloud 2019) show syscall-heavy workloads 2.8×–232× slower but CPU-bound workloads essentially unaffected. **Kata Containers** wraps each OCI container or Kubernetes pod in a lightweight VM (QEMU, Cloud Hypervisor, Firecracker or Dragonball backends) and is drop-in compatible with existing images — a dissertation-relevant property because the vulnerable images the LLM produces can be reused under Kata without rebuild. **Firecracker** (Agache et al., *Firecracker: Lightweight Virtualization for Serverless Applications*, USENIX NSDI 2020) is the minimal Rust-implemented VMM behind AWS Lambda and Fargate; by stripping QEMU's device model to virtio-net, virtio-block and a serial console and wrapping each process in a seccomp-filtered jailer, it achieves **sub-125 ms boot and under 5 MiB memory overhead per microVM**, making thousands of per-session microVMs per host practical. For fuller targets, **Proxmox VE** combines KVM/QEMU and LXC under a Debian host with integrated SDN (VLAN/VXLAN/EVPN zones), clustering via Corosync, and a REST API — a practical orchestration choice for academic and home-lab private clouds. **KubeVirt** brings KVM VMs into Kubernetes as custom resources, unifying pod and VM scheduling.

**Linux security primitives** combine defensively: seccomp-bpf constrains which syscalls can be attempted; capabilities constrain what a syscall grants; SELinux or AppArmor MAC constrains which labels/paths a process can touch (SELinux enforcing mode completely blocked CVE-2019-5736 on RHEL); **user namespaces** (Linux 3.8, 2013) remap container UID 0 to an unprivileged host UID and were the structural mitigation cited for the runc escape; and cgroups v2 enforce per-container memory, CPU, PID and I/O quotas that are essential against fork-bomb and memory-hog DoS. Podman's daemonless, rootless design eliminates the `docker.sock`-as-attack-surface class entirely, and LXC/Incus unprivileged containers default to user-namespace-mapped root. Kubernetes' Pod Security Standards (replacing PodSecurityPolicy in 1.25, 2022) codify the "Restricted" profile — `runAsNonRoot: true`, `allowPrivilegeEscalation: false`, seccomp `RuntimeDefault` — that is the minimum acceptable for a learner-facing workload.

**Network isolation** is the second half of the design. HackTheBox's public documentation describes the dominant pattern: per-user OpenVPN tunnels terminate on lab concentrators, push a `tun0` route into private 10.x lab subnets, and a gateway firewall restricts the learner's reachable IPs to their own assigned hosts; since January 2026 Free and VIP users receive dedicated Machine instances rather than shared VMs. WireGuard (in-kernel since Linux 5.6) offers the same topology with modern crypto and lower operational overhead for high-churn tenancy. Within orchestrators, **Cilium's eBPF datapath** provides identity-based L3–L7 NetworkPolicy (including `CiliumNetworkPolicy` CRDs that can allow `GET /api/users` while denying `POST /admin`) and kube-proxy replacement with O(1) service lookup; Calico offers comparable NetworkPolicy enforcement; VXLAN and EVPN extend L2 isolation across hosts. The invariant is a default-deny posture both between tenants and outbound to the internet — the latter prevents data exfiltration and RCE-to-cloud-metadata attacks from the deliberately vulnerable target itself.

For an ephemeral education platform the trade-off matrix is relatively crisp. Docker/runc with default hardening is fast and dense but unsafe for kernel-facing exploit code; Docker under gVisor is acceptable for user-space-only vulnerabilities such as web or application-level memory corruption; **Kata Containers on a Firecracker backend** gives VM-grade isolation with ~150 ms starts and retains OCI compatibility; direct Firecracker microVMs are the strongest cheap sandbox; full KVM/Proxmox VMs are necessary when the challenge itself requires kernel-level exploitation (rootkits, kernel CVE chains) but are too slow for sub-minute ephemerality. A hybrid — Kata by default on Kubernetes with Cilium and WireGuard, escalating to Firecracker microVMs or Proxmox VMs for kernel-space challenges — matches both the evidence and the AWS Lambda/Fargate design pedigree. A specific concern for LLM-authored images is **image supply-chain hygiene**: rejecting LLM-produced Dockerfiles that pin untrusted base images by tag rather than digest, that contain `WORKDIR /proc/...` patterns exploitable via CVE-2024-21626, or that specify unsafe `securityContext` overrides. The orchestrator, not the LLM, must be the source of truth for security context, enforced at admission by Kubernetes Validating Admission Policy or OPA/Gatekeeper.

## Where the proposed system extends the field

Four specific gaps in existing work jointly define the dissertation's contribution. **First, vulnerability content is universally static.** Every platform surveyed — HackTheBox, TryHackMe, picoCTF, VulnHub, OverTheWire, Root-Me, CyberDefenders, LetsDefend, PortSwigger, PentesterLab, Immersive Labs, RangeForce, SANS NetWars — publishes challenges whose vulnerability, codebase and flag are fixed at author-time. Per-user instancing, where it exists, serves isolation and availability rather than content variation, and write-ups propagate on IppSec's YouTube, 0xdf's blog, Medium and GitHub the moment retirement unlocks them. **Second, prior attempts at procedural generation are narrow.** APG (Burket et al. 2015), AutoCTF (Hulin et al. 2017), SecGen (Schreuders et al. 2017), ALPACA (Eckroth et al. 2019) and AgentCyTE (2025) demonstrate the pedagogical value of per-user variation but are template-based, domain-specific or limited to orchestrating pre-existing CVEs from repositories such as Vulhub. None synthesises novel vulnerable code or configurations end-to-end, and none provides **polymorphism** — the property that the same vulnerability class produces an observably different artefact every instantiation. **Third, existing AI features are tutors over static content, not generators of new content.** HTB's Guided Mode, LetsDefend's announced AI SOC, and the handful of academic CTF-hint systems (CTFMentorGPT, Hinrichs et al.) act on pre-authored challenges. No system yet integrates an LLM that is both the challenge's author and a privileged-but-guarded tutor grounded, via RAG and function calling, in exactly what it created. **Fourth, the hand-authored content pipeline is rate-limited.** HTB releases one Machine a week; picoCTF runs once a year; PortSwigger ships periodic batches. A generative pipeline collapses this bottleneck while simultaneously addressing the cheating and replay-value limitations documented by Vykopal et al. (2020) and Švábenský et al. (2021).

The dissertation's system — an LLM generator producing fresh per-session vulnerabilities in ephemeral sandboxed containers, a context-aware LLM tutor grounded in the specific generated artefact, user-tunable difficulty and vulnerability type, and destroy-on-disconnect semantics — therefore synthesises three mature but disjoint literatures. It inherits its safety model from NIST SP 800-190's ephemerality doctrine, Firecracker's microVM design and Kata's OCI compatibility; it inherits its generator viability from the Pearce/Perry/CyberSecEval/TitanFuzz/AIxCC demonstrations that LLMs produce, solve and patch CWE-listed vulnerabilities at scale; and it inherits its tutor architecture from the SocraticAI/CodeHelp/Khanmigo RAG-plus-guardrails pattern that contains prompt-injection and hallucination risk.

## Conclusion

The incumbent CTF and training-platform ecosystem is architecturally committed to static, pre-authored content, and the academic literature has repeatedly identified the pedagogical costs of that commitment — write-up memorisation, flag sharing, collapsed replay value and a rate-limited supply of fresh challenges. The LLM-in-cybersecurity literature has, in parallel, demonstrated that modern code-generating models reliably produce CWE-listed vulnerabilities, that agentic LLM systems can solve, fuzz and patch real-world bugs at near-human level, and that Socratic, RAG-grounded LLM tutors can scaffold learners without leaking solutions. Container, microVM and user-space-kernel technologies have matured to the point where deliberately vulnerable per-session workloads can be run in multi-tenant settings with hypervisor-grade isolation and sub-second ephemerality. The dissertation's contribution is to join these three literatures into a single pipeline — LLM-as-author, sandbox-as-substrate, LLM-as-grounded-tutor — and in doing so to occupy a gap that no commercial platform and no prior academic system has filled: **polymorphic, AI-generated, tutor-accompanied cybersecurity training at the granularity of a single learner session.**
