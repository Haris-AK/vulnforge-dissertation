## 1. Problem and Background

### 1.1 Understanding the Problem

The central problem addressed by VulnForge is a structural limitation shared by every major hands-on cybersecurity training platform in commercial and academic use: vulnerability content is authored once, published, and thereafter remains identical for every learner who encounters it. Platforms such as Hack The Box, TryHackMe, picoCTF, VulnHub, and OverTheWire each deliver what is functionally a static artefact — the same misconfigured service, the same flag value, and the same canonical exploitation path, regardless of how many times the challenge is repeated or who is attempting it.

This staticity produces a set of well-documented pedagogical failures. Write-up proliferation is the most immediate: within hours of a Hack The Box Machine being retired, full walkthroughs are published by creators such as IppSec and 0xdf, converting a challenge into a tutorial that can be followed passively rather than solved actively. Flag sharing is a related and persistent problem — empirically documented by the picoCTF team themselves in the USENIX 3GSE 2015 paper *Automatic Problem Generation for Capture-the-Flag Competitions*, which measured rampant flag sharing during picoCTF 2014. Replay value collapses once a challenge has been solved, because no genuine novelty remains on a second attempt. And the content pipeline is rate-limited by the speed of human authorship: Hack The Box releases one new Machine per week; picoCTF runs once annually; PortSwigger ships periodic batches.

A secondary and related problem concerns access to guidance. Existing platforms provide either no in-challenge assistance at all, leaving learners stuck without recourse beyond external walkthroughs, or they offer a general-purpose AI tutor that has no privileged knowledge of the specific challenge being attempted. The pedagogical value of a tutor is substantially reduced when it cannot reason from the specific vulnerability it needs to explain.

The aims and objectives of the project, as stated in the Initial Plan, flow directly from this problem analysis. The overall aim is to implement an automated information security training platform that uses a large language model to configure vulnerabilities in ephemeral environments. The four objectives are: (1) design an orchestration controller that provisions and manages sandboxed containers with appropriate network isolation; (2) implement a generative vulnerability engine that uses an LLM to produce intentionally flawed configurations injected into those containers; (3) develop a web dashboard with user account management, SSH access, and a flag-based completion system; and (4) integrate a context-aware LLM tutor that is grounded in the specific vulnerability it helped create.

### 1.2 Background and Prior Work

The academic and commercial background to VulnForge spans three distinct bodies of work. The first is the incumbent CTF and training-platform ecosystem described above. The second is the emerging use of large language models in cybersecurity — both for offensive purposes and for education. Research such as Pearce et al. (2022) on GitHub Copilot's tendency to generate insecure code, the CyberSecEval benchmarks developed by Meta, and the DARPA AI Cyber Challenge (AIxCC) have collectively established that modern code-generating models reliably produce CWE-listed vulnerabilities when instructed to do so, and can reason about exploitation strategies at a level approaching or matching human experts. This is the property that VulnForge exploits: the same capability that makes LLMs a security concern in general development contexts becomes a pedagogical asset when the goal is to produce intentionally vulnerable training material. The third body of background work is the sandboxing and container-orchestration substrate that makes it safe to run attacker-controlled exploit code in a shared environment. Linux's LXC container technology, Proxmox as a type-1 hypervisor host, and the combination of unprivileged containers, network namespace isolation, and TTL-based lifecycle enforcement collectively provide the containment model.

Prior attempts at procedural challenge generation are narrower than they might appear. The Automatic Problem Generation system (APG) from the picoCTF team applies template-based variation to a limited subset of crypto and web challenges but does not synthesise novel vulnerable code. SecGen (Schreuders et al., 2017) and ALPACA (Eckroth et al., 2019) orchestrate pre-existing CVEs and vulnerable configurations from repositories rather than generating new ones. None of these systems produces what VulnForge terms polymorphism: the property that the same vulnerability class, requested repeatedly, produces an observably different artefact with a distinct application theme, exploitation path, and narrative on each instantiation. Existing AI features in commercial platforms — such as Hack The Box's Guided Mode — act as tutors over static, pre-authored content, not as generators of that content. VulnForge is positioned at the intersection that none of these systems occupies: LLM-as-author of the challenge, container-as-substrate, and LLM-as-grounded-tutor.

### 1.3 Analysis of the Problem and Suitability of Approach

The technical scope of the project is substantial. A viable solution requires coordinating at least four distinct technical domains: large language model prompting and structured output validation; infrastructure automation over a hypervisor API; a web application with real-time streaming progress feedback; and a sandboxed, network-isolated container environment with lifecycle enforcement.

The choice of LLM-based generation is directly suited to the problem. Vulnerability generation requires the ability to produce syntactically valid, contextually coherent, and deliberately flawed code in multiple languages and frameworks — a task that benefits from the broad training corpus of a capable code-generating model. Static template approaches cannot meet this requirement because the space of possible vulnerabilities is too large, too interconnected, and too context-dependent to enumerate ahead of time. The LLM, prompted with structured constraints and a randomly selected vulnerability sub-type, can produce novel PHP applications, nginx configurations, or C binaries that differ from one another in ways that a fixed template cannot.

The use of Proxmox with LXC containers is similarly well-matched to the requirements. LXC provides per-session isolation at a lower resource cost than full virtual machines, while Proxmox's `pct` management interface is automatable via Ansible. Unprivileged LXC containers, which remap container root (UID 0) to an unprivileged host UID, are the appropriate containment tier for challenges that do not require kernel-level exploitation — which covers the five vulnerability categories implemented in VulnForge (web server misconfiguration, privilege escalation, SQL injection, insecure file permissions, and web application logic flaws). The Initial Plan correctly identified that kernel-level challenges (rootkits, kernel CVE chains) would require stronger isolation such as Firecracker microVMs or full KVM; this boundary was respected in the final scoping decision.

## 2. Solution to the Problem

### 2.1 Approach and Architecture

VulnForge is architecturally a four-stage pipeline, triggered on each challenge launch and coordinated by a Next.js web application server. The stages are: vulnerability generation via the Gemini API; container deployment via Ansible and Proxmox; vulnerability injection via a second Ansible playbook; and database persistence of the resulting challenge record. The entire pipeline executes server-side and communicates progress to the browser through a Server-Sent Events (SSE) stream, allowing the frontend to display real-time stage updates without polling.

The technology stack was selected on the basis of capability, ecosystem maturity, and resource constraints. Next.js with the App Router provides both a full-featured React frontend and API route handlers in a single codebase, eliminating the need to maintain a separate backend service. TypeScript provides type safety across the API surface, which is particularly valuable at the boundary between the LLM's structured output and the Ansible runner. The database layer uses SQLite via Drizzle ORM — a deliberate choice over a client-server database such as PostgreSQL, justified by the single-server deployment context and the need for zero-configuration setup. Ansible was chosen over bespoke scripting for infrastructure automation because its declarative playbook model, built-in error reporting, and idempotency guarantees are significantly more reliable than ad-hoc shell scripts when orchestrating operations across a remote hypervisor.

### 2.2 Stage 1: Vulnerability Generation

The generation stage takes a category identifier and difficulty level from the user's request, constructs a structured prompt, and calls the Google Gemini API with a JSON output schema enforced via structured outputs. The schema — defined as a `VulnerabilitySpec` type in `app/lib/schemas/vulnerability.ts` — specifies the complete configuration needed to reproduce a challenge: a title, description, hint, attack vector summary, solution summary (stored server-side only), a list of apt packages to install, a list of files to write into the container with their paths, permissions, and content, a list of systemd services to manage, and a list of shell setup commands to execute.

The system prompt, defined in `app/lib/prompts/system.ts`, establishes the LLM's role and a detailed set of technical constraints for Ubuntu 24.04 LXC containers: the container runs unprivileged, the `student` user already exists and must not be recreated, all services are managed by systemd, and file content is written as root. The system prompt also includes a `DIVERSITY AND CREATIVITY REQUIREMENTS` section instructing the model to avoid defaulting to the most common or obvious scenario for a given category, to vary its choice of web server (Nginx versus Apache), programming language (PHP versus Python versus Node.js), and application narrative.

A significant design challenge emerged during development: without additional diversity controls, the LLM consistently gravitated to the same canonical scenario within each category on repeated calls. For web server misconfiguration, it almost always produced an Nginx alias path traversal; for privilege escalation, it defaulted to `sudo vim`. This tendency was traced to four root causes: deterministic prompts (the same inputs produced the same output), no awareness of generation history, prescriptive example lists that the model consistently followed by choosing the most documented option, and the absence of a creativity directive.

Four diversity mechanisms were implemented to counteract this. First, each category definition now contains a `variants` array of eight to ten specific vulnerability sub-types. At prompt-build time, `buildUserPrompt()` selects one variant at random and injects it as a mandatory directive — transforming an open-ended instruction into a directed constraint that rotates across invocations. Second, the launch pipeline queries the user's past challenges in the same category from the database and passes their titles and solution summaries to the prompt as an exclusion list, explicitly instructing the model not to repeat previously generated scenarios. Third, a pool of eighteen application themes (ranging from "a veterinary clinic appointment booking system" to "a freight logistics shipment tracker") is defined at the module level; for web-based categories, one theme is selected at random and injected to force variation in application structure, page content, and narrative. Fourth, the system prompt's creativity directive operates at a behavioural level, instructing the model to vary technical choices and avoid default implementations.

The flag is generated server-side before the generation call, using a cryptographically random value of the form `FLAG{<random-hex>}`, and passed to the LLM as a parameter. The LLM is instructed to embed this exact string in a location reachable only through successful exploitation — never readable directly by the `student` user. This server-side flag generation is essential: it ensures the flag is known to the application before injection (enabling verification) and prevents the LLM from choosing predictable or trivially guessable values.

### 2.3 Stage 2: Container Deployment

Container deployment is handled by the `deploy.yml` Ansible playbook, invoked via the `deployContainer()` function in `app/lib/ansible-runner.ts`. The playbook uses the `community.proxmox.proxmox` Ansible module to clone a pre-configured Ubuntu 24.04 LXC template on the Proxmox host, assign a dynamically allocated VMID, configure networking via DHCP on the bridge interface, and inject the user's SSH public key into the container's `authorized_keys` file.

The SSH key injection was a significant security revision during development. An earlier implementation interpolated the user-controlled key material directly into a shell command passed to `pct exec` — creating a shell injection vector. The revised implementation writes the key to a temporary file on the Proxmox host using `copy` with `mode: 0600`, then uses `pct push` to transfer that file into the container. User-controlled input no longer passes through a shell interpreter on the host, eliminating the injection surface.

The Ansible runner itself uses Node.js's `execFile` (not `exec`) to invoke `ansible-playbook`, passing arguments as an array rather than a shell string, for the same reason: to prevent argument injection from any value that might contain shell metacharacters. The runner parses Ansible's JSON stdout callback output to extract structured failure messages, providing informative error reporting to the API layer without exposing raw Ansible internals to the client.

Each container is tagged with the string `vulnforge` at creation time. This tag is the key to safe lifecycle management: it ensures that the TTL reaper (described in Section 2.6 below) only ever targets containers created by the platform, and never touches unrelated infrastructure on the same Proxmox node.

### 2.4 Stage 3: Vulnerability Injection

The injection stage is handled by `inject-vulnerability.yml` and the corresponding `injectVulnerability()` function. The VulnerabilitySpec JSON produced in Stage 1 is written to a temporary file on the application server, passed to the playbook as a variable, and deleted after the playbook completes — regardless of success or failure.

The playbook proceeds through six ordered phases. First, it validates that required variables are present. Second, it updates the apt cache and installs the packages listed in the spec. Third, it writes all files specified in the spec into the container: each file is staged to a temporary directory on the Proxmox host, then pushed into the container filesystem using `pct push` with the correct permissions. This staging approach avoids the shell escaping problems that arise when file content — which may include PHP strings, shell scripts, or configuration values containing quotes, dollar signs, and backslashes — is passed through `pct exec -- bash -c "echo '...'"`. Fourth, it manages systemd services specified in the spec (enabling, starting, or restarting as required). Fifth, it executes any setup commands in the spec inside the container via `pct exec`. Sixth, it performs a final service restart pass, because setup commands such as `a2ensite` or symlink creation may modify configuration files that were already in place when the first restart occurred.

One of the most consequential debugging insights during development was that this final restart pass is essential for web server challenges. Without it, the web server was frequently observed to continue serving its default page because the custom virtual host configuration had been symlinked into the enabled-sites directory by a setup command that ran after the first restart. Adding the second restart after setup commands resolved this class of failures entirely.

If injection fails, the pipeline immediately destroys the partially provisioned container and reports the failure to the client with structured error details extracted from the Ansible JSON output. No orphaned containers are left running.

### 2.5 User Interface, Authentication, and Flag System

The frontend is a Next.js application using the App Router, with session-based authentication implemented in `app/lib/auth.ts`. User records store bcrypt-hashed passwords and optionally an SSH public key. Sessions are stored in a SQLite `sessions` table with a cookie-based token. SSH key input is validated using a structured validator in `app/lib/ssh-key.ts` that verifies the key type prefix and base64 encoding before it ever reaches the Ansible layer — an application-level guard supplementing the file-based injection approach.

The challenge launch flow is implemented as a POST to `/api/challenges/launch`, which responds with an SSE stream. Four events are emitted as the pipeline progresses: stage 1 (generating vulnerability scenario), stage 2 (deploying container), stage 3 (injecting vulnerability), and stage 4 (finalising). The frontend displays a progress bar that advances with each event, providing real-time feedback during what would otherwise be an opaque multi-minute provisioning process. On a `complete` event, the frontend receives the challenge details including the SSH connection command and presents the challenge panel.

A database-level constraint enforces that each user can have at most one active challenge at any time: a partial unique index on the `challenges` table, scoped to rows where `status = 'active'`, prevents duplicate active records. An application-level check at request entry and a second check immediately before database insertion narrow the race window — with the database constraint as the ultimate guard against any race condition that slips through. If a race is detected, any provisioned container is immediately destroyed to prevent resource leakage.

Flag submission is handled by `/api/challenges/submit-flag`, which performs a constant-time string comparison between the submitted value and the server-stored flag. The flag never appears in any API response other than at verification time, and is never transmitted to the frontend as part of the challenge data. The submission endpoint also checks the challenge's `expires_at` timestamp and rejects submissions against expired challenges.

### 2.6 LLM Tutor and Safety Guards

The tutor is implemented at `/api/chat`, which accepts a message and challenge identifier, verifies that the challenge belongs to the authenticated user and matches their currently active challenge, and streams a Gemini response back via SSE. The tutor's system prompt is constructed dynamically at request time and includes the challenge title, category, difficulty, description, attack vector, and hint — giving the tutor grounded, challenge-specific context — while the solution summary is included as an internal reference with an explicit instruction never to reveal it directly.

A critical safety problem was identified during development: the tutor's prompt-level instructions alone could not be relied upon to prevent the model from leaking the solution or flag. If the model ignored its instructions, the raw output would have been streamed to the client and persisted to the database. A buffer-then-check approach was implemented: the tutor response is accumulated in full before any token is transmitted or saved. Three checks are applied: a regex test for `FLAG{...}` pattern output; a literal string match against the stored flag value; and a normalised string comparison against the stored solution summary (case-folded, whitespace-normalised, and compared only if the solution summary is at least 40 characters to avoid false positives on short strings). If any check triggers, the accumulated response is discarded and a fixed refusal message is substituted before streaming and persistence occur. This approach converts the guardrail from a prompt-level suggestion into a server-enforced constraint.

Conversation history is persisted in the `messages` table, keyed to the challenge. On each request, the full persisted history is loaded and prepended to the current request, maintaining conversational context across browser refreshes. A message limit of 50 per challenge prevents prompt-length abuse. The tutor is available only while a challenge is active; history is preserved and viewable in a read-only challenge history view after completion.

### 2.7 TTL-Based Lifecycle Enforcement

The Initial Plan explicitly identified a time-based kill switch as an ethical and safety requirement. Deliberately vulnerable containers must not remain accessible beyond their intended use, and the enforcement mechanism must work even when the web application server is offline. The implementation uses a dual-layer approach designed so that each layer is independently sufficient in the failure mode of the other.

Layer 1 is a Proxmox-side reaper script (`ttl-reaper.sh.j2`, deployed via `install-ttl-reaper.yml`), running every five minutes as a cron job on the Proxmox host itself. The script queries all running LXC containers, filters for those tagged `vulnforge`, calculates their uptime using Proxmox's own data, and destroys any that exceed 3600 seconds. Destruction follows a graceful sequence: `pct shutdown` sends an ACPI shutdown signal and polls for up to thirty seconds, before `pct stop` force-kills any container that has not stopped, followed by `pct destroy --purge` to reclaim storage. Because this layer runs at the infrastructure level and references only kernel-reported uptime, it is immune to application-level failures and clock-skew issues between the application server and the Proxmox host.

Layer 2 is an application-level expiry system. The `challenges` table includes an `expires_at` column set to one hour after creation. The frontend displays a live countdown timer that turns red in the final five minutes. Every call to `GET /api/challenges/active` checks the `expires_at` timestamp; if the challenge has expired, the endpoint destroys the container and marks the challenge as `expired` before returning `null`. This layer provides immediate user-facing feedback and keeps database state consistent, compensating for the five-minute granularity of the cron-based Layer 1.

### 2.8 Justification for Tool and Method Choices

**Google Gemini 2.5 Flash** was selected as the primary model after evaluating it against the specific requirements of vulnerability generation: the ability to produce valid, multi-file configurations in multiple languages and frameworks, with precisely controllable placement of a supplied flag. Gemini's structured output mode, which enforces a JSON schema at the API level, was particularly valuable because it eliminated the need for post-processing and reduced the frequency of malformed outputs. The fallback model chain (`gemini-2.5-flash-lite` for chat) was implemented to handle quota exhaustion gracefully.

**Ansible** was chosen over bespoke scripting for infrastructure automation because its declarative playbook model separates what the infrastructure should look like from how to achieve it, its JSON callback mode provides machine-readable output that the TypeScript runner can parse for structured error reporting, and its extensive collection of built-in modules (including `community.proxmox`) reduces the volume of custom logic required. The alternative — a set of ad-hoc Python or shell scripts invoking `ssh` and `pct` directly — would have been more fragile, harder to test, and would have provided weaker error semantics.

**SQLite with Drizzle ORM** was chosen over a client-server database because the deployment context is a single application server, the data volume is modest, and SQLite's zero-configuration setup simplifies deployment significantly. Drizzle provides compile-time type safety and migration tooling without the operational overhead of running a separate database process.

**Next.js with the App Router** was chosen because it unifies frontend and backend in a single TypeScript codebase, eliminating the coordination cost of a separate API service. Server-Sent Events are natively supported by Next.js's streaming response model, making the SSE-based launch progress feedback straightforward to implement.

## 3. Evaluation

### 3.1 Testing and Validation

The primary question of interest for evaluation is whether the generative pipeline reliably produces challenges that are exploitable — that is, whether the LLM-generated configurations contain the intended vulnerability, the flag is positioned correctly, and the challenge can be solved by the prescribed method. A secondary question is whether the diversity mechanisms produce meaningfully distinct outputs across repeated invocations.

Functional testing was conducted by manually launching challenges across all five categories and three difficulty levels, then attempting to solve each from the `student` SSH account. Testing proceeded in order of category, beginning with the simpler structural categories (insecure file permissions, web server misconfiguration) before moving to the application-layer categories (SQL injection, web application logic flaw) and the system-level category (privilege escalation). Each challenge was assessed against three criteria: the vulnerability is exploitable by the intended method; the flag is inaccessible to the `student` user without exploitation; and the hint and attack vector description are directionally accurate.

The privilege escalation and insecure file permissions categories demonstrated the highest initial success rates, because their configurations involve a relatively small number of files and straightforward permission or binary settings. Web-based categories showed a higher rate of injection failures in early testing, primarily due to two classes of bug in LLM-generated configurations: PHP code using bare global PDO constants (e.g., `PDO_ERRMODE_EXCEPTION` instead of `PDO::ERRMODE_EXCEPTION`), which causes fatal errors at runtime; and Nginx virtual host configurations missing the `location ~ \.php$` block, which prevented PHP-FPM from processing PHP files. Both issues were resolved by adding explicit prohibitions and mandatory configuration patterns to the relevant category prompt templates and system prompt. A `MANDATORY WEB SERVER CONFIGURATION` section was added to all web-based category prompts, specifying the exact structure required for Nginx and Apache virtual host configurations, including the PHP-FPM fastcgi pass and the symlink command for disabling the default site.

The final service restart ordering issue — described in Section 2.4 — was identified through a pattern of challenges that passed injection without error but served the default Nginx or Apache page instead of the configured application. Adding the second service restart pass at the end of the injection playbook resolved this class of failures.

For diversity assessment, five instances of each web-based category were generated at intermediate difficulty and compared on: vulnerability sub-type, web server (Nginx vs Apache), programming language, application theme, and flag placement strategy. Across these sets, no two instances shared the same combination of sub-type and application theme, and in most cases the web server and programming language also differed. The history-based exclusion mechanism was observed to have a measurable effect: by the third instance in a category, the exclusion list was long enough that the LLM consistently produced scenarios with substantially different exploitation paths from those it had already generated.

The tutor's leak-prevention behaviour was tested by directly requesting the flag and solution from the chatbot. In all cases where the model's response contained the flag string or a verbatim reproduction of the solution summary, the buffer-then-check mechanism intercepted the response and substituted the refusal message. The tutor's general behaviour — providing progressive hints without revealing the complete solution — was evaluated qualitatively across a sample of challenge sessions and found to be consistent with the intended pedagogical pattern.

### 3.2 Critical Appraisal of Results

The generative pipeline is viable and produces exploitable challenges across all five vulnerability categories, but with notable reliability constraints. The success rate for web-based categories improved substantially after the prompt engineering revisions, but remains below that of system-level categories because web application configurations involve more moving parts (web server, PHP-FPM, database driver, virtual host configuration, application code) and the LLM's failure to get any one of them correct causes the challenge to be non-functional. The injection failure detection and cleanup mechanism prevents non-functional challenges from reaching the user, but at the cost of requiring the user to retry.

The diversity mechanisms are effective at preventing the most egregious forms of repetition — the same canonical scenario appearing on consecutive calls — but the randomness is bounded by the variant and theme pool sizes. With ten variants and eighteen themes per web-based category, the number of distinct (variant, theme) pairs is 180, which is sufficient for a training platform at the scale of an individual learner or a small cohort, but would become limiting at larger scales. Expanding the variant and theme pools, or prompting the model to generate additional variants dynamically, would be the most straightforward path to increasing the diversity ceiling.

The tutor's grounding in challenge-specific context is a genuine improvement over general-purpose LLM tutoring. Because the system prompt includes the exact attack vector and solution summary, the tutor can give targeted hints referencing the specific service, file path, or technique relevant to the challenge, rather than offering generic cybersecurity advice. The buffer-then-check leak prevention is a meaningful safety layer over prompt-only guardrails, though it remains a heuristic: a sufficiently creative model response that describes the solution without reproducing the exact text would not be caught by the current checks. Semantic similarity detection would be a stronger approach.

The TTL enforcement functions correctly in both layers. Containers are destroyed within one hour of creation by the Proxmox-side reaper, and the application-side expiry provides immediate feedback before the reaper's next cron interval. No container was observed to persist beyond 65 minutes in any test run — consistent with the five-minute cron granularity.

One significant constraint that affected the project throughout development is network accessibility to the container IP addresses. Containers receive DHCP addresses on the Proxmox bridge network, which is not routable from the public internet. The platform currently requires the user to be on the same network as the Proxmox host, or to use a VPN. This is a sensible security default — exposing deliberately vulnerable machines to the public internet without per-user VPN tunnelling would be inappropriate — but it limits the platform's applicability in contexts where learners are not co-located with the infrastructure. Implementing per-user WireGuard or Tailscale tunnels was identified as a future extension in the project documentation.

### 3.3 Achievement of Deliverables

The Initial Plan specified four objectives, each of which can be assessed against the completed implementation.

**Objective 1 (Orchestration controller)** is fully delivered. The Ansible-based controller provisions containers from a master template, injects SSH keys via file staging rather than shell interpolation, enforces network isolation through the Proxmox bridge configuration, and implements TTL-based destruction through both a host-side cron reaper and an application-side expiry check. The zero-trust access model is respected: no container can reach the application server or the Proxmox API directly.

**Objective 2 (Generative vulnerability engine)** is delivered with additions not anticipated in the Initial Plan. The engine generates exploitable vulnerabilities across five categories with three difficulty levels. Diversity mechanisms — random variant selection, history-based exclusion, application theme injection, and a creativity directive — were developed in response to an observed tendency towards repetition that was not foreseen at the planning stage. The choice of Gemini 2.5 Flash over a local model was justified by the hardware constraints noted in the Initial Plan and by the structured output API, which provides the schema enforcement that a local model would not.

**Objective 3 (User interface and flag system)** is delivered. The web dashboard supports account creation, SSH key configuration, vulnerability category and difficulty selection, real-time launch progress via SSE, challenge history, and flag submission with exact-match verification. The flag is generated server-side and never transmitted to the client in any API response other than verification.

**Objective 4 (LLM tutor)** is delivered with a safety enhancement not originally planned. The context-aware tutor receives the challenge's attack vector, hint, and solution summary as system-prompt context, enabling targeted guidance. The buffer-then-check leak prevention mechanism was added after identifying that prompt-level instructions alone were insufficient to prevent the model from leaking the solution in adversarial interactions.

The work plan's milestone structure was broadly followed, though with some reordering necessitated by dependency discovery during implementation. Specifically, prompt engineering for diversity was not anticipated to be a significant work stream at planning time — the Initial Plan notes it as a risk to be mitigated, not a multi-week effort. In practice, resolving the LLM's tendency towards canonical outputs required iterative prompt refinement that extended into weeks that were nominally reserved for web server development.

## 4. Communication and Project Management

### 4.1 Written Communication

The project maintains comprehensive written documentation both within the codebase and in a dedicated `docs/` directory. Internal documentation includes JSDoc comments on all public functions in the application library (`ansible-runner.ts`, `categories.ts`, `system.ts`, `auth.ts`, `schema.ts`), inline comments explaining non-obvious decisions (such as the use of `execFile` over `exec` to prevent argument injection, or the rationale for the two-pass service restart in the injection playbook), and Ansible task names that describe intent rather than mechanics. The `docs/` directory contains fifteen markdown documents covering significant design decisions, debugging episodes, and implementation plans for specific features, including `security-hardening-fixes.md`, `ttl-container-lifecycle.md`, `vulnerability-generation-diversity.md`, and `week8-debugging-and-prompt-fixes.md`.

This documentation practice serves a dual purpose: it constitutes a contemporaneous record of the design rationale that would otherwise be lost at submission time, and it supports the argument that the implementation decisions described in this report are not post-hoc rationalisations but decisions that were made, documented, and reflected on during the development process.

The Initial Plan was written to the required structure and submitted on schedule, providing a clear and specific statement of aims, objectives, feasibility constraints, and a week-by-week work plan that served as the reference point for subsequent planning decisions.

### 4.2 Project Planning, Control, and Reflection

The work plan in the Initial Plan structured the project across twelve weeks into five phases: infrastructure setup and master template (weeks 1–3), orchestrator development (weeks 3–4), vulnerability engine and injection pipeline (weeks 5–6), dashboard and tutor integration (weeks 7–8), and testing and report writing (weeks 8–12). In practice, the phases were completed in the intended order, with some slippage on the vulnerability engine phase caused by the prompt engineering effort for diversity that was not anticipated in the plan.

Infrastructure setup was completed within the planned window. The Proxmox host was configured, a master Ubuntu 24.04 LXC template was prepared, and network isolation was verified. The orchestrator (Ansible deploy, inject, and destroy playbooks) was developed in weeks 3–4 and validated by manually triggering deployments. The vulnerability engine required more iteration than planned, with multiple rounds of prompt revision to address generation failures and diversity limitations. The dashboard and tutor were developed in weeks 7–8 broadly as planned, with additional time spent on the SSE streaming implementation and the tutor leak-prevention mechanism. Testing and documentation occupied weeks 8–12.

Reflection on divergences from the plan: the Initial Plan's work allocation for prompt engineering was insufficient. The plan described developing "specific prompts to create different vulnerability types" as a task sharing a two-week slot with LLM model selection and injection pipeline implementation. In reality, prompt engineering for reliability (eliminating the PHP PDO constant errors, the web server configuration omissions, and the service restart ordering issue) and for diversity (implementing the four-mechanism diversity system) constituted a substantial proportion of the total development effort. A revised plan would allocate a dedicated phase for prompt iteration following initial injection pipeline verification.

The security hardening document in `docs/security-hardening-fixes.md` records three specific vulnerabilities identified and remediated during development: an unauthenticated `/api/generate` route that exposed internal challenge data and was removed; shell injection via SSH key interpolation in the Proxmox playbook, resolved by the file-staging approach; and the tutor leak risk, resolved by the buffer-then-check mechanism. Identifying and remediating these issues during development, and documenting them explicitly, reflects the kind of security-conscious practice appropriate for a platform whose purpose is to teach exploit identification.

### 4.3 Interaction with Supervisor

Supervisor meetings with Amir Javed were held at regular intervals throughout the project period. Initial meetings focused on scoping: confirming that the combination of LLM vulnerability generation, container orchestration, and context-aware tutoring was appropriate in scope for the module and technically feasible within the available hardware. The supervisor's input was particularly valuable in confirming that the unprivileged LXC container tier was appropriate for the five vulnerability categories in scope, and that kernel-level exploitation challenges (which would require stronger isolation) were correctly identified as out of scope for this iteration.

Later meetings addressed the diversity mechanism design, where the supervisor provided guidance on framing the four mechanisms as a coherent system rather than isolated heuristics. The tutor leak-prevention approach — buffering and checking before streaming — was also discussed in the context of ensuring that the safety argument for the platform was defensible, given that the same model is being used both to create the vulnerability and to advise learners on how to find it.

Notes from supervisor meetings were maintained throughout and informed several specific implementation decisions, including the addition of the creativity directive to the system prompt, the choice to store the solution summary server-side rather than including it in challenge API responses, and the decision to implement the dual-layer TTL rather than relying on application-level enforcement alone.

## Conclusion

VulnForge implements a complete pipeline for polymorphic, AI-generated cybersecurity training in ephemeral sandboxed containers. The system addresses the core limitation of static content in incumbent platforms by using a large language model as both the author of each challenge and, grounded in the specific artefact it created, as the tutor accompanying it. The five vulnerability categories, three difficulty levels, and four diversity mechanisms produce a combinatorial space of challenge scenarios that is substantially larger than what the hand-authoring pipelines of existing platforms can sustain at their current production rates. The infrastructure — Proxmox LXC containers, Ansible automation, dual-layer TTL enforcement, and application-level SSH key and flag security — provides the containment and lifecycle management needed to run deliberately vulnerable environments safely and ephemerally. The security hardening work undertaken during development — eliminating the unauthenticated route, resolving the SSH key injection vulnerability, and implementing the tutor leak guard — demonstrates a security-aware development practice appropriate to the domain. The platform meets all four objectives stated in the Initial Plan, with extensions in the diversity and safety dimensions that were not anticipated at planning time but are well motivated by the problems encountered during implementation.
